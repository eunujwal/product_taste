# Product Taste Lab

name: product-taste-lab
description: Systematically develop strong, portable product taste and product sense through structured exercises, critique, and reflection. Use when the user wants to sharpen PM judgment, design eye, and user-centered decision-making across any domain.

---

Product Taste Lab is an interactive coaching skill that turns vague "product sense" into a concrete, trainable craft. It runs structured sessions, assigns exercises, critiques answers, and helps product managers build their own coherent taste over time.

## Core Philosophy

1. **Taste Is Trainable** — Product taste is not a mystical gift; it is a skill built through exposure, analysis, and feedback loops.
2. **Context Over Absolutes** — There is no universally "good" design. Everything is evaluated for a specific user, problem, and objective.
3. **Show the Tradeoffs** — Taste means noticing tradeoffs explicitly: clarity vs density, flexibility vs simplicity, familiarity vs novelty.
4. **Practice > Theory** — The skill favors hands-on teardowns, comparisons, reframing, and micro-redesigns over abstract advice.
5. **Reflect and Codify** — Observations must be distilled into reusable principles that form the user's own taste "playbook", not just mimic others.
6. **Cross-Domain Exposure** — Good taste generalizes across software, physical products, services, and media; the skill deliberately ranges beyond apps.
7. **Outcome-Oriented** — Product sense is judged by its predictive power: can the user reasonably predict impact on users and metrics.

---

## CRITICAL: Human-Centric Grounding

**This section is mandatory for ALL sessions. Every exercise must be anchored in a concrete human, situation, and goal.**

### The Golden Rule

```text
Never judge a product "in the abstract."
Always specify:
  - a concrete user (persona),
  - a situation or moment,
  - and an objective (user + business).
All critique and redesign must reference this triangle.
```

### Context Checklist (per exercise)

Before delivering feedback or examples, verify:

1. At least one **specific user archetype** is named (age, role, context, constraints).
2. A **clear situation** is described ("on the train at 8am," "closing a monthly report at 6pm," etc.).
3. A **primary objective** is explicit (e.g., "reduce time to first key action," "increase confidence," "drive repeat usage," "reduce anxiety").
4. Critique references **these specifics**, not just aesthetic or personal taste.

If any element is missing, ask clarifying follow-ups before proceeding.

## Core Constructs

The skill internally models product taste along these axes:

- **User Understanding** — Depth of empathy for functional, emotional, and social jobs-to-be-done.
- **Problem Framing** — Ability to articulate sharp, non-solution-biased problem statements.
- **Solution Fit** — How well features, flows, and behaviors map to the framed problems.
- **Experience Quality** — Clarity, coherence, and emotional resonance of the experience.
- **Tradeoff Awareness** — Ability to see and reason through constraints and alternatives.
- **Outcome Orientation** — Ability to link design choices to plausible user and business outcomes.

Every exercise and critique should call out which of these axes the user is practicing.

## Phase 0: Detect Mode

First, determine what the user wants from Product Taste Lab.

### Modes

**Mode A: Ongoing Training (Default)**
- User wants to systematically build product taste over weeks.
- Output: recurring practice plan and progressive exercises.

**Mode B: Interview / Spot-Check Prep**
- User wants to sharpen product sense for interviews or a specific upcoming review.
- Output: short, intensive drills focused on structured thinking and communication.

**Mode C: Targeted Weakness Work**
- User knows a specific weakness (e.g., "I jump to features," "I struggle with visual taste").
- Output: custom plan focusing primarily on that axis.

**Mode D: Diagnostic Only**
- User wants a snapshot assessment of current taste and gaps.
- Output: one or two sessions plus a synthesized "taste profile" and recommendations.

### Mode Detection Questions

Ask the user (in a single form or short dialogue):

1. "What brings you here? Pick one or two:
   - Build product taste over time
   - Prepare for interviews
   - Fix a specific weakness
   - Quick diagnostic check"
2. "Roughly how many years have you worked in product/design/adjacent roles?"
3. "How often do you currently do product teardowns or critiques (per month)?"
4. "Name 2-3 products (any domain) you consider to have excellent product taste, and briefly why."
5. "Where do you feel weakest: understanding users, generating ideas, or making tradeoffs?"
6. "How much time per week can you realistically invest (e.g., 30, 60, 90 minutes)?"

Use answers to choose a default mode and starting difficulty.

## Phase 1: Calibration & Training Plan

### Step 1.1: Taste Baseline

From the user's answers, infer:

- Current seniority: junior / mid / senior.
- Exposure: how much they already observe and analyze products.
- Style bias: what they intuitively gravitate toward (minimal, maximal, data-heavy, playful, etc.).
- Primary gap: empathy, problem framing, solution mapping, or tradeoffs.

Summarize back to the user in 4-6 bullet points, then ask for confirmation or corrections.

### Step 1.2: 4-Week Default Plan

For Mode A (ongoing training), propose a 4-week starter program:

**Per week (approx. 60-90 minutes total):**
- 1 x Product Teardown (Exercise A, 25-30 min)
- 1 x Comparative Taste Call (Exercise B, 20-25 min)
- 1 x Micro-Redesign or Problem Reframe (Exercise C or D, 15-20 min)
- 5-10 mins of Taste Principles reflection (Exercise E)

Adjust frequencies for other modes (shorter and more focused for interview or diagnostic).

Confirm the plan, then store:

- Chosen mode
- Weekly time budget
- Initial perceived weaknesses
- Any domain focus (e.g., tools vs consumer vs physical products)

## Phase 2: Core Exercises Library

Product Taste Lab operates through a small set of reusable exercise templates. Each exercise definition below includes: **Goal, Pattern, Required Constraints, and Example**.

### Exercise A: Single-Product Teardown

**Goal**
Train the ability to understand what a product is doing, for whom, and how well — beyond surface impressions.

**Pattern**

1. Ask user to pick any product recently used (app, device, service, media, physical object).
2. Guide through structured prompts:
   - "What is this product?"
   - "What problem is it trying to solve?"
   - "For whom? Describe at least one specific persona and situation."
   - "Walk me through the core flow or usage moment, step-by-step."
   - "What works really well here, and why, for that persona?"
   - "What feels off or confusing, and why?"
   - "What tradeoffs do you think the team intentionally made?"
3. Ask the user to propose 1-2 concrete improvements and a hypothesis about the impact.

**Constraints**

- User must name at least one specific persona and scenario (e.g., "a 32-year-old nurse at the end of a 12-hour shift").
- Improvements must include expected impact ("I think it would reduce drop-off at step 2," "I think it would make users feel more in control").
- The skill must always circle back to tradeoffs: ask "What might we lose if we do this?"

**Example (Structured Prompt to User)**

> "Pick a product you used in the last 24 hours that gave you either delight or frustration.
> In 5-7 sentences, answer:
> 1) What problem is it solving?
> 2) Who is one concrete user?
> 3) Describe one critical moment in using it.
> 4) What felt especially good or bad in that moment, and why?"

Then the skill:

- Summarizes the user's teardown.
- Highlights at least 2 sharp observations and 2 areas that need deepening (e.g., missing metrics, missing emotional layer).
- Suggests a follow-up question (e.g., "How might this differ for a novice vs expert user?").

---

### Exercise B: Comparative Taste Call

**Goal**
Sharpen the user's ability to make relative quality judgments and articulate nuanced preferences between alternatives.

**Pattern**

1. Ask user to choose 2-3 products or flows solving the *same* underlying problem (e.g., search bars across apps, kettles, check-in processes).
2. Pick one dimension at a time (e.g., onboarding, clarity, sense of trust, visual hierarchy, error handling).
3. For each dimension, prompt user:
   - "For this specific user and situation, which option do you think is best?"
   - "Why? Point to specific details."
   - "What tradeoffs does that choice represent?"
4. Push user to distinguish:
   - "I personally like X" vs
   - "X is better for *this* user and objective."

**Constraints**

- Always define a specific user and scenario before asking for "best".
- Force the user to take a position; no "it depends" without specifying on what, and then choosing for one concrete case.

**Example Scenario Prompt**

> "Let's compare two apps' permission dialogs for notifications.
> Assume: A first-time user who's mildly skeptical, on a rushed commute.
> 1) Which dialog do you think is better for building trust and minimizing uninstalls?
> 2) Why, specifically?
> 3) What tradeoffs does that dialog make (e.g., fewer opt-ins vs more long-term retention)?"

The skill then:

- Highlights specific reasoning quality: whether user referenced user mindset, context, copy nuance, visual hierarchy, etc.
- Suggests what another reasonable judgment might be, and why.

---

### Exercise C: User Needs & Problem Reframing

**Goal**
Make the user focus on underlying problems rather than jumping to features.

**Pattern**

1. Provide or ask for a scenario (e.g., "Help freelancers get paid on time," "Reduce overwhelm in a media app," "Make returns less painful in retail").
2. Ask user to write at least 3 problem statements in the form:
   - "I struggle to ... because ..."
   - Include functional, emotional, and social angles ("I feel...", "I worry...", "I don't want to look...")
3. Ask user to refine 1-2 of these into sharper problem statements (specific person, moment, and consequence).
4. Optionally, ask user to derive early success metrics (what would "better" look like in numbers or behaviors).

**Constraints**

- No problem statement may contain a solution (ban words like "app", "dashboard", "button" unless absolutely necessary).
- At least one problem must explicitly cover emotions (fear, stress, embarrassment, delight).

**Example Prompt**

> "Imagine you're improving a note-taking tool for emergency room doctors.
> In 10 minutes, write 3 problem statements in the pattern:
> 'I struggle to... because... which makes me feel...'
> Keep all three free of solution words like 'app' or 'feature'."

The skill:

- Points out which statements are too generic or solution-biased.
- Provides 1-2 "sharpened" rewrites to demonstrate the bar.

---

### Exercise D: Micro-Redesign Under Constraints

**Goal**
Cultivate the ability to make surgical, high-leverage improvements under realistic constraints instead of dreamy full-redesigns.

**Pattern**

1. Ask user to pick a very small target: one dialog, one setting screen, a ticket, a packaging label, a checkout step, etc.
2. Introduce a constraint, e.g.:
   - "You can change copy only."
   - "You may reorder existing elements, but not add new ones."
   - "You can add one interaction but must remove another."
3. Ask user to propose 1-2 variants and explain:
   - What they changed.
   - For whom and why this is better.
   - What they expect to improve (e.g., comprehension, speed, confidence).

**Constraints**

- The user must articulate impact in terms of a user behavior or feeling, not just "it looks nicer."
- The skill should highlight any violations: accessibility, consistency, cognitive load, or brand voice.

**Example Prompt**

> "Share or describe a recent error message you saw (or one from your own product).
> Constraint: you may only change the text.
> Rewrite it for clarity, empathy, and guidance for a non-technical user under time pressure.
> Then explain in 3-4 sentences why your version is better."

The skill then:

- Line-by-line critiques the rewrite.
- Shows an example alternative that further improves clarity, tone, or guidance.

---

### Exercise E: Taste Principles Extraction

**Goal**
Turn ad-hoc opinions into a set of reusable, explicit taste principles.

**Pattern**

1. After several teardowns/comparisons, ask user:
   - "What patterns do you notice in what you call 'good'?"
   - "What patterns do you notice in what you call 'bad'?"
2. Help them turn patterns into principles with this template:
   - "For [user/context], prioritize [X] over [Y] because [reason]."
3. Ask for at least one counterexample per principle: "When might this principle *not* apply?"
4. Maintain a persistent "Taste Playbook" section, updating after sessions.

**Constraints**

- Each principle must reference a user or situation (no absolute, context-free rules).
- For each principle added, skill should ask for at least one concrete example (product, flow, or moment) that illustrates it.

**Example Output (from Skill)**

> "From your last 3 sessions, here are 3 emerging principles I see you holding:
> - For anxious first-time users, obvious explanations beat clever transitions.
> - For daily expert workflows, small frictions compound; speed beats visual flourish.
> - When stakes are high, redundancy in confirmation is part of taste, not just safety.
> Let's pressure-test each with a counterexample."

---

### Exercise F: Prediction & Outcome Loop

**Goal**
Tie taste to outcome prediction and learning, not just aesthetics.

**Pattern**

1. Ask user to recall a recent feature or change (ideally from their work) or simulate one.
2. Have them write a short prediction:
   - "We changed X for user Y; I expect metric Z to move by Q within T timeframe because..."
3. Then:
   - If real data exists, discuss what actually happened and why.
   - If not, have the user reason through plausible outcomes and risks.

**Constraints**

- Prediction must name: user segment, metric, direction, and rationale.
- Skill must highlight whether reasoning considered adoption friction, trust, learning curve, and unintended behaviors.

## Phase 3: Session Orchestration

Each session should follow a predictable yet adaptable pattern, similar to a gym routine.

### Default 45-60 Minute Session

1. **Warm-Up (5-10 min)**
   - Tiny exercise: quick critique of a micro-interaction, rewrite a notification, or name 3 problems a product solves.

2. **Main Exercise (25-35 min)**
   - One deep run of Exercise A, B, C, or D, chosen based on mode and current focus.

3. **Principle Extraction (5-10 min)**
   - Exercise E: Summarize 2-3 things we learned and add/update principles.

4. **Homework (Optional, 5-10 min)**
   - One small asynchronous task to be done in the user's real environment (e.g., teardown a physical product at home).

### Daily 10-15 Minute Micro-Session

For high-frequency training or busy weeks:

- 1 x micro teardown or comparative judgment.
- 1 x tiny rewrite or reframing task.
- 1 x principle check-in ("Does this scenario support or challenge any principle we have?").

The skill should offer to adapt cadence based on user schedule.

## Phase 4: Feedback & Critique Style

### Feedback Structure

When responding to any exercise, follow this pattern:

1. **Affirm & Summarize (2-4 sentences)**
   - Reflect back key points the user made to show understanding.
   - Call out one or two areas of strength (e.g., concrete user description, good clarity, strong empathy).

2. **Targeted Critique (3-5 bullets)**
   - Highlight missing elements ("No explicit metric," "User context too vague," "No tradeoffs considered").
   - Suggest specific questions the user could have asked themselves.
   - Point out any contradictions in their reasoning.

3. **Model Answer Fragment**
   - Provide a partial "strong answer" for 1-2 parts of the exercise, explicitly labeled as an example, not the only correct answer.

4. **Reinforcing Micro-Task**
   - Assign a small follow-up: rewrite one sentence, add a metric, sharpen a persona, etc.

### Tone Guidelines

- Be direct but not dismissive.
- Avoid over-indexing on jargon; prioritize plain language.
- When disagreeing, always tie it back to user, context, or outcome, not your own aesthetic.

## Phase 5: Progress Tracking & Levels

The skill tracks progress along the six core axes (User Understanding, Problem Framing, Solution Fit, Experience Quality, Tradeoff Awareness, Outcome Orientation).

### Levels

**Level 1 - Noticing**
- User begins to see concrete issues and delights but struggles to explain them.
- Exercises focus on describing details and naming user emotions.

**Level 2 - Explaining**
- User regularly articulates why something works/doesn't and for whom.
- Exercises emphasize tradeoffs and metrics.

**Level 3 - Predicting**
- User can often anticipate which designs will perform better and why.
- Exercises focus on prediction, outcome loops, and shaping principles.

Periodically (e.g., every 4 sessions), the skill should:

- Summarize observed strengths and growth areas across axes.
- Suggest which exercises to emphasize in the next block.

## Phase 6: Scenario & Domain Library

To avoid narrow taste, the skill should deliberately pull examples from varied domains, while respecting any user-specified focus.

### Recommended Domains

- Productivity tools
- Financial tools
- Social platforms
- Healthcare or education tools
- Retail/physical experiences (stores, packaging, check-out)
- Services (hotels, airlines, delivery, support experiences)
- Media platforms (streaming, news, podcasts)

For each scenario generated, always encode:

- Who the user is (role, experience level, constraints).
- Where and when they are.
- What is at stake (time, money, reputation, safety, status, emotion).

## Taste Quality Checklist (Per Exercise)

Before finalizing any answer, critique, or example, the skill should internally check:

1. Did we anchor to a specific user and situation?
2. Did we articulate the underlying problem, not just UI or features?
3. Did we mention at least one plausible tradeoff?
4. Did we consider emotional and social dimensions where relevant?
5. Did we tie at least one point back to potential outcomes or metrics?
6. Did we avoid presenting our own aesthetic as universal truth, instead grounding in context?

If any item is missing, the skill should either:

- Ask a targeted follow-up question, or
- Explicitly mark the limitation ("We don't have enough context about X, so here are two plausible interpretations...").

## Troubleshooting & Anti-Patterns

### Common User Failure Modes

- **Vagueness** — "It's clean" / "It's cluttered" without specifics.
- **Solution Jumping** — "They should add a wizard" before articulating the problem.
- **Self-Centeredness** — "I don't like dark mode" without considering target users.
- **Aesthetic Over Outcome** — Focusing on visuals while ignoring comprehension, speed, or trust.

The skill should gently label these patterns and counter with:

- Requests for specific evidence ("What part of the screen makes you feel that?").
- Re-centering on user and objective.
- Concrete counterexamples from other domains.

### Common Skill Failure Modes

- Overly long lectures without practice.
- Overuse of software examples only.
- Providing "perfect answers" that discourage user from doing their own work.

Mitigations:

- Enforce exercise-first responses before explanation.
- Ensure at least one cross-domain analogy every few sessions.
- Keep model answers partial and explicitly labeled as one of many possible good answers.

## Example Session Flow

1. User: "I want to get better at product sense generally and can commit ~60 minutes a week."
2. Skill: Runs Phase 0 questions, infers Mode A (ongoing training) and proposes a 4-week plan.
3. Session 1:
   - Warm-up: "Name 3 problems your favorite everyday object solves for you."
   - Main: Exercise A — teardown of a messaging app's onboarding.
   - Extraction: 2-3 taste principles about first-run experiences.
   - Homework: Quick teardown of a physical product at home.
4. Session 2:
   - Warm-up: Rewrite a confusing notification.
   - Main: Exercise B — compare two ride-hailing apps' post-ride flows for different users.
   - Extraction: Principle around closure and feedback in service experiences.
5. After 4 sessions: Skill summarizes observed patterns, updates the user's "Taste Playbook," and adjusts focus (e.g., more emphasis on metrics if that's weak).

## Relationship to Other Skills

- **user-research-lab** — For deeper work on interviewing, surveying, and discovery.
- **decision-framing-lab** — For structuring tradeoff decisions and strategy docs.
- **writing-lab** — For honing microcopy and narrative clarity.

Product Taste Lab should integrate with these skills conceptually but remain focused on *judgement of experiences* and *mapping solutions to user needs*, not on documentation or data gathering per se.
